White Dwarf

A white dwarf is a stellar core remnant composed mostly of electron-degenerate matter. These remnants form when a star of low to medium mass (up to about 8 solar masses) exhausts its nuclear fuel and expels its outer layers as a planetary nebula.

White dwarfs are incredibly dense: one teaspoon of white dwarf material would weigh tons on Earth. They no longer undergo fusion but remain hot and glow for billions of years before gradually cooling into a black dwarf â€” a process longer than the current age of the universe.

Sirius B, the companion of the bright star Sirius, is one of the most well-known white dwarfs. These stars provide critical clues in understanding stellar evolution and the future of our Sun.
